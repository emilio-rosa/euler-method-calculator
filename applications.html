Application 1 — Finance

Pricing Derivatives & Modelling Asset Prices

From Black-Scholes to the Heston model, modern quantitative finance depends on numerically solving stochastic differential equations — and Euler is almost always where you start.

The Problem

A stock price does not move smoothly — it jumps, drifts, and behaves randomly. Mathematicians model this randomness with a stochastic differential equation (SDE), which is like an ODE but with a noise term added. You cannot solve most SDEs with a pen and paper, so you simulate thousands of possible price paths numerically.

📈

Black-Scholes Model (1973)

Assumes a stock price S follows geometric Brownian motion — a constant drift μ (expected return) plus random noise σ (volatility). It gives a closed-form option price formula, but its derivation relies on solving the underlying SDE numerically to check boundary conditions and Greeks.

🌀

Heston Model (1993)

Extends Black-Scholes by making volatility itself random — it follows its own mean-reverting SDE. No closed-form solution exists for most derivative prices under Heston, so numerical simulation (via Euler-Maruyama) is essential.

🎲

Euler-Maruyama Method

The direct stochastic extension of Euler's method to SDEs. It is the simplest and most widely used method for simulating random processes in finance, physics, and biology. You almost certainly used a version of it in your first quant finance interview problem.

The Black-Scholes SDE

Under Black-Scholes, a stock price S obeys:

Black-Scholes geometric Brownian motion dS = μS dt + σS dWt

where μ is the drift (expected return), σ is the volatility, and dWₜ is a Wiener process — pure random noise scaled by √dt. The term σS dWₜ is what makes this stochastic and unsolvable by standard Euler.

The Heston Model — Two Coupled SDEs

Heston adds a second SDE for the variance v (volatility squared), which mean-reverts toward a long-run level θ:

Heston model — coupled stochastic system dS = μS dt + √v · S dW(1)t
dv = κ(θ − v) dt + ξ√v dW(2)t

κ is the mean-reversion speed, θ is the long-run variance, ξ is the volatility of volatility ("vol of vol"), and the two Wiener processes W⁽¹⁾ and W⁽²⁾ are correlated with coefficient ρ. This captures the famous volatility smile that Black-Scholes cannot.

Euler-Maruyama: Euler's Method for Stochastic Equations

For a general SDE dX = f(X,t) dt + g(X,t) dW, Euler-Maruyama discretises it at time steps Δt:

Euler-Maruyama update rule Xn+1 = Xn + f(Xn, tn) Δt + g(Xn, tn) · √Δt · Zn

where Zₙ ~ N(0,1) is a fresh standard-normal random draw at each step. The first two terms are exactly standard Euler's method applied to the drift. The third term adds the scaled random noise. Applied to Black-Scholes this becomes:

Euler-Maruyama applied to Black-Scholes Sn+1 = Sn + μSn Δt + σSn · √Δt · Zn
Connection to your calculator: Remove the σSₙ · √Δt · Zₙ noise term and you have exactly the deterministic Euler step your calculator computes. Euler-Maruyama is literally Euler's method with one extra line of randomness.

How a Monte Carlo Option Pricer Works

  • 1 Simulate 10,000 stock price paths from today to expiry using Euler-Maruyama.
  • 2 For each path, compute the option payoff at expiry (e.g. max(S−K, 0) for a call).
  • 3 Average the 10,000 payoffs and discount back to today at the risk-free rate.
  • 4 That average discounted payoff is the fair option price. More paths = more accuracy, just like smaller h in Euler.
Industry context: Investment banks run Euler-Maruyama simulations millions of times a day for risk management (Value at Risk), exotic option pricing, and regulatory stress tests. The J.P. Morgan RiskMetrics framework and most Bloomberg derivative pricing models use variants of this exact method.
Application 2 — Physics & Engineering

Orbital Mechanics & Rocket Trajectories

NASA and SpaceX use numerical ODE solvers — built on the same ideas as Euler's method — to plan every burn, orbit insertion, and landing.

The Problem

Newton's second law gives you acceleration, not position directly. To know where a spacecraft is you have to integrate twice: acceleration → velocity → position. For anything beyond a simple two-body problem (Earth + satellite) there is no closed-form solution, so you step through time numerically.

🛰️

Orbital Mechanics

A satellite's position and velocity are governed by a system of six coupled ODEs (x, y, z positions and velocities). Euler's method — or more accurately its higher-order descendants like Runge-Kutta 4 — steps these forward in time to predict where the satellite will be hours from now.

🌍

N-Body Gravity

When multiple bodies pull on each other (Sun + planets + moons), the forces update at every step. The Solar System simulator on your phone is doing Euler-style time-stepping over millions of small Δt intervals.

Newton's Law as a First-Order System

Newton gives us F = ma, so a = F/m. Acceleration is the second derivative of position, so we split it into two first-order ODEs — exactly the kind Euler's method handles:

Two coupled first-order ODEs for one dimension dx/dt = v ← velocity is the rate of change of position
dv/dt = F(x, v, t) / m ← force divided by mass gives acceleration

Euler's method steps both equations forward simultaneously: xₙ₊₁ = xₙ + vₙ Δt and vₙ₊₁ = vₙ + (F/m) Δt. For a satellite orbiting Earth, F is the gravitational force −GMm/r² pointing toward Earth's centre.

Why not plain Euler for real missions? Euler's method has first-order accuracy. For a 6-month Mars transit, even tiny per-step errors compound into errors of millions of kilometres. NASA uses high-order Runge-Kutta methods and symplectic integrators, but they are all extensions of the same step-and-repeat idea that Euler invented.

The Pendulum — A Nonlinear ODE

A simple pendulum of length L has the nonlinear ODE d²θ/dt² = −(g/L) sin θ. Split into two first-order equations and apply Euler's method — this is a classic textbook example where you can watch the approximation drift away from the true oscillation as step size increases.

Pendulum as a first-order system dθ/dt = ω ← angular velocity
dω/dt = −(g/L) sin(θ) ← angular acceleration
Try it in the calculator: Enter −sin(x) (approximating θ ≈ x) with x₀ = 0, y₀ = 0.5, and watch how the curve changes as you slide h. At large h the oscillation grows unboundedly — a classic Euler instability.
Application 3 — Biology & Epidemiology

Epidemic Modelling & Population Dynamics

The COVID-19 forecasts that appeared on government dashboards every day were produced by numerical ODE solvers running models that are direct descendants of Euler's method.

The Problem

How does a disease spread through a population? How do predators and prey keep each other in check? These processes are described by systems of coupled first-order ODEs — which Euler's method can step through time directly.

🦠

SIR Epidemic Model

Divides a population into Susceptible (S), Infected (I), and Recovered (R). Three coupled ODEs describe how people move between groups over time. Used to model flu, measles, COVID-19, and every major pandemic forecast.

🐺

Lotka-Volterra Predator-Prey

Models the cyclical rise and fall of two species — for example wolves and rabbits. The rabbit population explodes when wolves are scarce, which then feeds a wolf boom, which crashes the rabbits, which then starves the wolves. Eternal cycle, described by two ODEs.

The SIR Model Equations

Let S, I, R be the fractions of the population in each compartment (so S + I + R = 1). The model is:

SIR — three coupled first-order ODEs dS/dt = −βSI ← susceptibles are infected at rate β
dI/dt = βSI − γI ← new infections minus recoveries (rate γ)
dR/dt = γI ← recovered = removed from infectious pool

Applying Euler's method to the I equation: Iₙ₊₁ = Iₙ + (βSₙIₙ − γIₙ) · Δt — exactly the same update rule as your calculator, just with a different f(x, y).

The Basic Reproduction Number R₀

The ratio R₀ = β/γ tells you whether an epidemic will grow or die out. If R₀ > 1 the infected compartment grows; if R₀ < 1 it shrinks. COVID-19 had an R₀ of roughly 2.5–3 for the original strain and 8–15 for Omicron. Every lockdown policy was designed to push R₀ below 1 by reducing β.

Real-world scale: The Imperial College London model that triggered the UK's first COVID lockdown in March 2020 was a highly extended SIR model numerically integrated forward in time using exactly this stepping approach, run on a supercomputer for hundreds of thousands of population sub-groups simultaneously.

Lotka-Volterra (Predator-Prey)

Lotka-Volterra — predator-prey system dx/dt = αx − βxy ← prey grow at α, eaten at rate βy
dy/dt = δxy − γy ← predators grow eating prey, die at rate γ
Try the prey equation in the calculator: Enter 0.6*y − 0.4*x*y with x₀ = 0, y₀ = 2, h = 0.1. You will see the first half-cycle of the population oscillation — the same curve ecologists use to model real wildlife data.

Why Euler Is Still Used Here

  • 1 Speed of prototyping. When building a new model, scientists code up Euler first to check the equations make sense before switching to a more accurate solver.
  • 2 Stochastic SIR. Real outbreaks are random — especially in small populations. Adding noise to the SIR equations (like Euler-Maruyama does for finance) gives stochastic epidemic models that match observed data far better.
  • 3 Real-time forecasting. Euler's method is simple enough to run in a spreadsheet, which means public health teams in low-resource settings can still use it during an outbreak without a supercomputer.
